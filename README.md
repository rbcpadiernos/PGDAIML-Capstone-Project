# Licensure-exam-success-prediction

This project aims to develop a machine learning model that predicts whether a student will pass or fail a licensure examination based on mock exam performance and learning behavior.
This serves as an early warning system for educational institutions and review platforms (e.g., BoardPrep) to intervene and improve student outcomes.

Problem statement
This study aims to develop a machine learning model to predict licensure examination outcomes using mock exam performance and learning behavior data. In licensure review programs, students often lack objective measures of readiness, resulting in inefficient study strategies and suboptimal passing rates. For education platforms such as BoardPrep, the inability to identify at-risk students early limits opportunities for targeted intervention and improved cohort performance, directly affecting overall success rates and learner outcomes.
The dataset consists of longitudinal student-level interactions with review materials, which are aggregated into features capturing performance (e.g., first, last, and best scores), improvement trends, and engagement patterns. The problem is formulated as a supervised binary classification task, as the target variable is categorical (pass or fail in the licensure examination). Model performance will be evaluated using ROC-AUC as the primary metric (target: >0.75) to assess overall discriminative ability, with F1-score and recall as secondary metrics. Recall is prioritized to ensure that at-risk students are correctly identified for early intervention. The intended business outcome is to enable early prediction and targeted support strategies, with the goal of improving licensure examination passing rates and enhancing the effectiveness of review programs.

Dataset source and description
The dataset used in this study was sourced from an internal database of Board Prep Solutions Incorporated, specifically from the 2025 Veterinary Licensure Review Cohort. It consists of aggregated, student-level data capturing mock exam performance and related learning behaviors. The data were systematically extracted from the BoardPrep Classroom platform, where students engage in assessments, practice drills, and review activities. Each record represents a consolidated view of a student’s performance across multiple mock exams, enabling analysis of patterns and predictors associated with licensure exam outcomes.

Results summary
This study developed and evaluated multiple machine learning models to predict Veterinary Licensure Examination (VLE) outcomes using student-level mock exam performance data. Several classification algorithms were tested, including Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest, and XGBoost. After applying hyperparameter tuning and cross-validation, the Random Forest model demonstrated the best overall performance, achieving an approximate ROC-AUC score of 0.80. This indicates strong discriminative ability between students who will pass and those at risk of failing. The model also maintained a balanced trade-off between precision and recall, making it suitable for early warning applications where both accurate identification of successful students and detection of at-risk individuals are important.
Further evaluation using the confusion matrix showed that the model correctly identified the majority of students who eventually passed the licensure examination, while maintaining relatively low false negatives. This is particularly critical in the context of intervention systems, where failing to identify at-risk students could result in missed opportunities for academic support. Although some false positives were observed, this is considered acceptable within the use case, as early intervention systems are designed to prioritize sensitivity over strict precision.
Feature importance analysis and SHAP (SHapley Additive Explanations) interpretation revealed that baseline academic performance is the most influential factor in predicting licensure success. In particular, the average first attempt score emerged as the strongest predictor, highlighting the importance of early academic readiness. Subject-specific performance, especially in Zootechnics, also contributed significantly to prediction outcomes. Additionally, measures of consistency, such as score variability and stability indices, were found to be critical, with students exhibiting lower variability in scores demonstrating a higher likelihood of passing. Improvement trends over time, captured through engineered features such as average score improvement, further reinforced the importance of learning trajectory rather than relying solely on static performance metrics.
Exploratory data analysis supported these findings by showing that higher average and best scores are strongly associated with successful licensure outcomes. Students who demonstrated consistent performance across multiple attempts and subjects tended to outperform those with fluctuating scores. Moreover, repeat examinees exhibited distinct performance patterns, suggesting that prior takes of licensure exam and engagement behavior can provide additional predictive value.
Overall, the results demonstrate that mock examination data can serve as a reliable predictor of licensure outcomes. The model not only provides strong predictive performance but also offers interpretability through explainable AI techniques, enabling actionable insights for educators. These findings support the development of an AI-powered early warning system that can identify at-risk students early in the review process, allowing for targeted interventions and improved licensure success rates.
